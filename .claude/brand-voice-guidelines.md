## Executive Summary

F.A.S. Motorsports is a three-generation, family-owned performance shop in Punta Gorda, Florida, operating since 2002. The brand voice is that of a working shop that earns its reputation on the dyno, not in a copywriter's office. Every claim is anchored to something provable: a part that ships from the same floor that installs it, a horsepower number verified on the dyno before keys are handed back, a supercharger ported by the same hands that designed it.

The voice is direct, technical, and confident without being loud. It speaks peer-to-peer — enthusiast to enthusiast — with the specificity that only comes from actually doing the work. The shop doesn't need to perform expertise because it has a 24-year paper trail of builds to stand behind. Copy reads like it came from the shop floor, not a marketing department: short, declarative, precise about materials and process, indifferent to hype.

F.A.S. stands for **Fast. Aggressive. Superior.** — a tagline that also functions as a standard. Every piece of content should pass that test before it ships.

### Voice Attributes Detail

#### Attribute 1: Technical

- **What it means**: F.A.S. speaks with part-level precision. Not "a supercharger upgrade" — "a race-ported 2.7L Eaton with a 108mm throttle body and longtube headers." The specificity is the proof.
- **How it shows up**: Material callouts (304/316L stainless), process callouts (TIG welded, race-ported), model-specific references (Hellcat, TRX, LT4), package feature lists that name every component.
- **What to avoid**: Adjective-heavy copy with no nouns behind it. "High-flow intake" is fine as a component name; "high-flow air system for better breathing" is not.
- **Evidence**: PowerPackages.astro feature lists: "Race-Ported Supercharger / 108mm Throttle Body + High-Flow Intake / Longtube Headers + Race Midpipes / E85 Fuel System + ID Injectors / Custom Dyno Tune"; CustomFabricationSection: "304 and 316L stainless work, TIG welded and fitted in-house."
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 2: Direct

- **What it means**: Short sentences. Active voice. No qualifiers unless they're technical ones ("91/93 octane," "5-10 business days"). If a number exists, use it. If a claim is true, state it flat.
- **How it shows up**: Hero headline is three words on three lines: "Fast. / Aggressive. / Superior." Service descriptions are one clause: "Airflow-focused head and intake work matched to target power." Packages name a price: "From $6,499."
- **What to avoid**: Run-on sentences. Passive voice. Words like "perhaps," "potentially," "we believe," "world-class."
- **Evidence**: HomepageHero h1: "Fast. / Aggressive. / Superior." ServicesStrip service descriptions: all under 12 words each. PowerPackages sub: "Every package is built with parts we designed and manufactured. Every install happens in our shop. Every number gets proven on the dyno before your keys come back."
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 3: Legacy-Backed

- **What it means**: Three generations of actual racing history. Frank Mingione II set the culture. Frank III scaled it. Frank IV integrated it vertically. The timeline is real and the brand earns its confidence from this lineage — it doesn't just claim expertise.
- **How it shows up**: "Est. 2002" in the hero kicker. "Three Generations. One Standard." The StorySection headline: "Built by Racing. Carried by Family." Stats: 50+ years combined, 500+ builds.
- **What to avoid**: Vague "decades of experience" phrasing that anyone can write. Always anchor to the specific timeline or specific person.
- **Evidence**: StorySection.astro timeline (Frank II, Frank III, Frank IV narratives), StatsBand (50+ years combined, 2002), hero kicker "Punta Gorda, FL · Est. 2002."
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 4: Vertically Integrated / No Subcontractors

- **What it means**: FAS designs, manufactures, installs, and tunes under one roof. This is the single most defensible competitive differentiator. It needs to appear everywhere it's relevant — not as a boast, but as a statement of how the shop operates.
- **How it shows up**: "The installs are done by the same people who designed and machined the parts." "Every billet part ships from the same shop that installs it." Service kickers: "Everything Done In-House." Custom Fab kicker: "In-House."
- **What to avoid**: Describing this as "convenient" or "one-stop-shop." Frame it as precision and accountability, not convenience.
- **Evidence**: StorySection: "No subcontractors, no fitment guesswork — just shop-floor experience applied to every car that rolls in." PowerPackages sub: "Every package is built with parts we designed and manufactured. Every install happens in our shop."
- **Confidence**: High

#### Attribute 5: Dyno-Proven / Evidence-First

- **What it means**: Every HP claim has a number, and every number has a dyno pull. FAS does not say "significant power gains" — it says 800 HP, 1,000 HP, 1,500 HP. The numbers are the brand.
- **How it shows up**: Package names are the HP number (FAS800, FAS1000, FAS 1X). The PowerPackages section sub says explicitly "Every number gets proven on the dyno before your keys come back."
- **What to avoid**: Range-padding language ("up to X HP"), superlatives without numbers ("massive power increase"), unverified claims.
- **Evidence**: Package naming convention (FAS800, FAS1000, FAS 1X / 1500 HP). PowerPackages sub copy. SEO Report: "record-breaking builds with dyno data."
- **Confidence**: High

## Messaging Framework (Confidence: Medium-High)

### Primary Value Proposition

F.A.S. Motorsports is the only performance shop in the Southeast that designs, manufactures, installs, and dyno-tunes from the same floor — so every number on the spec sheet is something the shop is personally accountable for.

Variations observed:
- "Three generations of racing and fabrication, now focused on billet parts, in-house installs, and validated power packages for the builds that actually get driven hard." (HomepageHero subcopy)
- "F.A.S. Motorsports grew out of a real racing legacy — not a drop-ship catalog." (StorySection)
- "The installs are done by the same people who designed and machined the parts." (StorySection)

### Key Message Pillars

1. **In-House Everything**
   - Core idea: Design → manufacture → install → tune under one roof, with personal accountability at every step.
   - When to use: Lead with this on any page that competes with national resellers or multi-vendor shops. Always relevant in the About, Services, and any package page.
   - Example phrasing: "No subcontractors. No fitment guesswork. The same hands that machined the part install it."
   - Confidence: High

2. **Dyno-Proven Numbers**
   - Core idea: HP claims are not estimates — they are verified on-site before delivery. FAS names the number because it can back it.
   - When to use: Any package description, product spec sheet, or performance claim. Never state a HP figure without the dyno-proven qualifier in at least one adjacent touchpoint.
   - Example phrasing: "Every number gets proven on the dyno before your keys come back."
   - Confidence: High

3. **Racing Legacy → Shop Standard**
   - Core idea: The performance standard was set on a race track, not in a catalog. Three generations carried it into fabrication and manufacturing.
   - When to use: About page, homepage story section, any context where trust or credibility is the goal.
   - Example phrasing: "Built by racing. Carried by family."
   - Confidence: High

4. **Platform-Specific Expertise**
   - Core idea: FAS is not a generalist shop. They have deep, model-specific knowledge of Hellcat, TRX, LT4, F-150, Powerstroke, and Jeep Trackhawk platforms. That specificity is proof of expertise.
   - When to use: Any platform landing page, product description, or search-focused content. Use the full model name (Dodge Challenger SRT Hellcat, not just "Challenger") and specific part callouts.
   - Example phrasing: "We built the FAS 2.7L race-port program for Hellcat specifically — not adapted from a universal kit."
   - Confidence: Medium

5. **Transparent Pricing**
   - Core idea: FAS shows prices. Competitors say "call for pricing." This is a trust differentiator and a CTR signal. Lean into it.
   - When to use: Package pages, service pages, any SEO content where trust is competing with national brands.
   - Example phrasing: "Pricing is on the site. No games."
   - Confidence: Medium

### Competitive Positioning

- vs. Ripatuned: Ripatuned resells — FAS manufactures. Ripatuned doesn't touch diesel. FAS does Hellcat + diesel + fabrication under one roof.
- vs. Kong Performance: Kong is single-service (CNC porting). FAS offers porting + billet manufacturing + fabrication + diesel — a complete build solution.
- vs. Regional generalist shops: FAS has platform-specific programs (named packages, specific HP targets) developed over 500+ builds, not generic "performance upgrades."
- vs. Status Quo (stock vehicle): The customer is leaving HP on the table that the factory intentionally restricted. FAS unlocks what the platform was designed to make.

## Tone-by-Context Matrix (Confidence: Medium)

Voice is constant. Tone flexes by context.

| Context | Formality | Energy | Technical Depth | Key Principle |
|---------|-----------|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Homepage hero | Low-Medium | High | Low | Three words if you can. Make them count. |
| Package / product page | Medium | Medium | High | Spec everything. Let the numbers close. |
| Services page | Medium | Medium | Medium | Name the process. State the outcome. |
| Blog / content | Medium | Medium | High | Teach something real. No fluff. |
| GBP description | Low-Medium | Medium | Medium | Keywords + proof + CTA. No filler. |
| GBP posts | Low | Medium-High | Low | Vehicle-specific. Outcome-focused. Short. |
| About / story | Medium | Low-Medium | Low | Let the history speak. Don't oversell it. |
| Q&A / FAQ | Low | Low | High | Answer the actual question. Completely. |
| Product review ask | Low | Low | Low | Personal, brief, specific to their vehicle. |

### Context-Specific Guidelines

#### Package / Product Pages
- **Overall tone**: Technical and confident. The customer is already interested — close with specs, not marketing.
- **Opening approach**: Name the HP target. Then list what gets you there.
- **Do's**: Full component list. Material callouts. Dyno-proven qualifier. Price on the page.
- **Don'ts**: "Take your build to the next level." "You won't be disappointed." "Best in class." Anything without a number behind it.
- **Example**: "FAS1000 — 1,000 HP. Race-ported supercharger, 108mm throttle body, longtube headers, E85 fuel system, ID injectors, and a custom dyno tune. Full install in-shop. From $16,999."

#### GBP Posts
- **Overall tone**: Direct, vehicle-specific, action-oriented. These are SEO assets as much as marketing.
- **Opening approach**: Lead with the vehicle + service completed. Specific is always better.
- **Do's**: Name the vehicle model. State what was done. Include CTA + phone. Use location keywords naturally.
- **Don'ts**: Generic "check out our latest work!" Emojis as the primary communication vehicle. Vague outcomes.
- **Example**: "Just installed an IGLA anti-theft system on a Dodge Hellcat here in Punta Gorda. IGLA locks the engine and transmission through the CAN bus — no wiring cuts, no subscriptions, invisible to thieves. Call (812) 200-9012 to book yours."

#### About / Brand Story
- **Overall tone**: Restrained confidence. The legacy speaks for itself — copy shouldn't oversell it.
- **Opening approach**: Start with a fact, not an adjective. "Three generations" is a fact. "Passionate team" is noise.
- **Do's**: Specific names (Frank II, III, IV). Specific dates. Specific capability milestones.
- **Don'ts**: "Passion for performance." "We love what we do." "Family values." State the fact; let the reader infer the value.

#### Blog / Content
- **Overall tone**: Peer-to-peer. You're a builder talking to another builder who already knows what a Hellcat is.
- **Opening approach**: Answer the search intent in the first paragraph. No throat-clearing.
- **Do's**: Dyno data. Part numbers. Process steps. Comparisons with real specs.
- **Don'ts**: Intro paragraphs that restate the title. Filler sections. "In conclusion." Generic recommendations.

## Terminology Guide (Confidence: High)

### Must-Use Terms
| Term | Usage | Instead Of | Example |
|------|-------|------------|---------|
| **Dyno-proven** | Always qualify HP claims | "estimated," "up to," "potential" | "800 dyno-proven HP on pump gas" |
| **In-house** | Describe manufacturing and install | "in-store," "on-site" | "machined and installed in-house" |
| **Race-ported** | For supercharger porting work | "modified," "upgraded," "improved" | "race-ported 2.7L Eaton" |
| **Billet** | For CNC-machined aluminum parts | "custom," "aftermarket," "performance" | "FAS billet supercharger lid" |
| **F.A.S. Motorsports** | Full brand name on first reference | "FAS," "the shop" (on first use) | "F.A.S. Motorsports is a certified IGLA installer" |
| **Certified installer** | For IGLA service specifically | "authorized," "official" | "certified IGLA installer serving Southwest Florida" |

### Preferred Terms
| Term | Usage | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Platform** | Referring to a vehicle model family | "the Hellcat platform," "the TRX platform" |
| **Build** | A customer's completed performance project | "500+ builds since 2002" |
| **Power package** | Named FAS HP program (FAS800, FAS1000, FAS 1X) | "the FAS1000 power package" |
| **Custom tune / dyno tune** | ECU calibration work | "custom dyno tune included" |
| **Punta Gorda, FL** | Location — always city + state | "our shop in Punta Gorda, FL" |
| **Southwest Florida** | Regional service area | "serving all of Southwest Florida" |
| **CAN/LIN bus** | IGLA technical context | "integrates with the vehicle's CAN/LIN bus" |

### Avoid These Terms
| Term | Reason | Alternative |
|------|--------|-------------|
| "World-class" | Unverifiable, overused | Cite the actual record or stat |
| "Premium" (standalone) | Generic, meaningless | Name the material or process |
| "Passionate" | Every shop says this | Show the 24 years and 500 builds |
| "One-stop shop" | Retail-speak, undermines the manufacturing differentiator | "vertically integrated" or "design, machine, install, tune — under one roof" |
| "Next-level" | Marketing copy, zero information content | State the number |
| "Solutions" | Corporate filler | Name the actual service |
| "Excited to announce" | Marketing theater | Just announce it |

### Never-Use Terms
| Term | Reason |
|------|--------|
| "Game-changing" | Empty superlative, signals copy over substance |
| "Revolutionary" | Same — overused to the point of meaning nothing in automotive |
| "Best in class" | Requires third-party citation to mean anything |
| "Industry-leading" | Same — either cite the record or don't claim it |

## Language to Avoid (Confidence: High)

### Anti-Patterns
1. **"Take your build to the next level"** — Problem: Generic, zero information, could be any shop. Better: Name the HP target and what gets you there.
2. **"We're passionate about performance"** — Problem: Every shop says this. Better: "500+ builds since 2002. Dyno sheet on every one."
3. **"High-quality parts"** — Problem: Adjective without a noun. Better: "304 stainless TIG-welded in-house" or "FAS billet lid CNC-machined from 6061 aluminum."
4. **"Call for pricing"** — Problem: Erodes trust. FAS shows prices as a differentiator — don't undo it. Better: Show the price. If truly variable, show a range with "custom consultation" for top-tier builds.
5. **"Results may vary"** or hedged HP claims — Problem: Undermines the dyno-proven positioning. Better: State the actual dyno number. If it varies by tune/fuel, state both: "800 HP on 93 octane / 850 HP on E85."
6. **Excessive emoji in professional content** — Problem: Undermines technical authority. Better: Reserve emoji for GBP social posts only, and use sparingly (1 per post max).

### Examples to Avoid

**Generic automotive marketing copy:**
> "We're passionate about helping you get the most out of your vehicle with our premium performance solutions."

---

**Vague service description:**

*Why it's off-brand:* "Experts" without credential, "work with you" without process, "right upgrade" without spec. Better: "Tell us the platform and the HP target. We'll spec the package and give you a price — on the site or over the phone."
